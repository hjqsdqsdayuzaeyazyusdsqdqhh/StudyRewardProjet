Medical Devices in Clinical Trials: What to Know

How medical device clinical trials differ from drug trials, including regulatory pathways and what participants should expect.

By Dr. James Wilson January 28, 2026

How medical device clinical trials differ from drug trials, including regulatory pathways and what participants should expect.

How Device Trials Differ from Drug Trials

Medical device clinical trials follow a different regulatory pathway than drug trials. Devices are classified by risk level, with Class I devices requiring the least oversight and Class III devices requiring the most rigorous testing. Unlike drugs, devices do not go through the same phased approach and are often evaluated through feasibility studies followed by pivotal trials. The focus is on safety, performance, and usability rather than pharmacokinetics.

Types of Device Studies

Device trials include feasibility studies testing early prototypes, pivotal trials that generate data for FDA approval, post-market studies monitoring long-term performance, and comparative effectiveness studies that evaluate a new device against existing options. Some device trials are randomized and blinded, though blinding can be challenging when the device requires a visible surgical procedure or physical presence.

What Participants Should Know

Participants in device trials may need to undergo training on using the device and keep detailed logs of their experience. Follow-up periods vary depending on whether the device is implanted or used externally. Implantable devices typically require longer monitoring to assess long-term safety and function. Participants should understand what happens if the device malfunctions and whether explant surgery would be covered.

Key Takeaways

  • Device clinical trials follow risk-based classification rather than the phased approach used for drugs.
  • Feasibility and pivotal trials are the main stages of device evaluation.
  • Blinding can be more difficult in device trials due to the physical nature of the intervention.
  • Participants with implantable devices may require extended follow-up monitoring.
