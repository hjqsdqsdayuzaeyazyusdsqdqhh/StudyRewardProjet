Understanding Clinical Trial Results and Publications

Learn how to read and interpret clinical trial results, understand publications, and know what study findings mean for patients.

By Dr. Sarah Mitchell February 14, 2026

Learn how to read and interpret clinical trial results, understand publications, and know what study findings mean for patients.

How Clinical Trial Results Are Published

After a clinical trial concludes, researchers analyze the data and submit their findings to peer-reviewed medical journals. The publication process involves independent experts reviewing the study design, methodology, and conclusions before acceptance. Results are also typically posted on ClinicalTrials.gov within one year of study completion, ensuring public access to the findings regardless of whether the results are positive or negative.

Key Statistical Terms to Understand

Understanding a few basic statistical concepts helps make sense of trial results. Statistical significance indicates whether the observed effect is likely due to the treatment rather than chance. Confidence intervals show the range within which the true effect likely falls. The p-value measures the probability that results occurred by random chance, with values below 0.05 conventionally considered statistically significant.

Interpreting Efficacy and Safety Data

Efficacy results tell you how well the treatment worked under ideal conditions, while safety data reveals the types and frequency of side effects. Look for the number of participants who experienced each side effect and how severe those effects were. Compare the treatment group results with the placebo or standard care group to understand the true benefit. Also note whether the study was large enough and long enough to detect meaningful differences.

Key Takeaways

  • Clinical trial results are published in peer-reviewed journals and posted on ClinicalTrials.gov.
  • Statistical significance, confidence intervals, and p-values are key concepts for interpreting results.
  • Efficacy and safety data should be reviewed together to understand the full picture.
  • Comparing treatment group results to control group results reveals the true treatment effect.
